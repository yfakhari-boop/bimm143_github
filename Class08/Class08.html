Q8. Generate a similar plot for principal components 1 and 3. What do you notice about these plots?

The PC1 vs PC3 plot shows less clear separation between malignant and benign samples compared to the PC1 vs PC2 plot. While PC1 still captures the main variation and helps distinguish the groups, PC3 does not add much additional separation. This suggests that PC2 is more informative than PC3 for distinguishing between the two diagnoses, and most of the important structure in the data is captured by PC1 and PC2.

Variance explained

A scree plot shows how much variance each PC captures. We typically look for an “elbow” — a point where adding more PCs gives diminishing returns. This can help us decide how many PCs to consider for further analysis.

Calculate variance of each component

pr.var <- wisc.pr$sdev^2
head(pr.var)
[1] 13.281608  5.691355  2.817949  1.980640  1.648731  1.207357
pve <- pr.var / sum(pr.var)

plot(c(1, pve), 
     xlab = "Principal Component", 
     ylab = "Proportion of Variance Explained", 
     ylim = c(0, 1), 
     type = "o")
barplot(pve, 
        ylab = "Percent of Variance Explained",
        names.arg = paste0("PC", 1:length(pve)), 
        las = 2, 
        axes = FALSE)

axis(2, at = pve, labels = round(pve, 2) * 100)

The loading of concave.points_mean on the first principal component is approximately 0.26, indicating it contributes strongly to PC1.
